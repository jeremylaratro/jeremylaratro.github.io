Bromine

- - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

Please do not try this at home.

2NaBr + H2SO4 + H2O2 = Br2 + Na2SO4 + 2H2O

Bromine is a halogen, with symbol Br and atomic number 35. Bromine is quite toxic and extreme caution must be used when handling it. Because of this, many labs have transitioned to producing bromine in-situ when performing addition reactions or multi-step reactions where bromine is necessary or preferred. Due to the nature of Bromine, it is extremely useful in a wide array of reactions, including substitution, elimination, and addition reactions where a bromine is needed, or where a bromine is added in order to then be used as a leaving group in multi-step syntheses (ie. forming alkenes or alkynes, or vice versa).

First, safety precautions were made. Anything can be dangerous, but a well-constructed emergency plan can make the difference between an accident and a tragedy. Since I do not have the luxury of professional-grade equipment, I created a simple plan that would be easy to follow in the case of any incident. I believe this is a crucial step when doing anything with the possibility of danger, as a common trend among most of the lab accidents that I've read about, is failure to abide by the safety protocols once panic has set in. A simple, easy to follow list of steps to take for the most-likely incidents allows for quick reference, and removes the possibility of forgetting or overlooking a procedure in panic. These steps can be as simple as:

"Glass break / spill: immediately pour Sodium Thiosulfate to neutralize the bromine, then evacuate area until fumes have cleared."

Or, "Fume leak: immediately stop heating, exit area, and wait for fumes to clear."

Various methods of physical safety were also implemented, including fans and a commercial exhaust system, connected to ducting placed strategically aside the distillation apparatus. As in a regular lab, a lab coat, gloves, goggles, and pants were worn to ensure that no skin was exposed, and due to the lack of a commercial fume hood, a respirator with sufficient acid-gas cartridge was also worn.

For this extraction, I used simple distillation. Reagents used were as follows:

1. Sodium Bromide (NaBr): 70g

2. Sulphuric Acid (H2SO4): 20mL

3. Water (H2O): 35mL

4. Hydrogen Peroxide (H2O2): 30mL (~35%)

Reagents and apparatus were all prepped before starting. This includes "greasing" the glass joints with H2SO4 (as Bromine is highly reactive, and some greases can cause complications. An inverted funnel trap was also implemented to ensure that any gaseous bromine was able to escape safely. The trap was terminated in a shallow dish containing concentrated sodium thiosulfate in distilled water. Once everything was in place, the distillation process was started. 70g of NaBr and 35mL of H2O were added to the round bottom flask, followed by 20mL of H2SO4. An addition funnel was then added with 30mL of H2O2, and the H2O2 was added at a rate of ~1 drop per 2 seconds. This immediately resulted in Br2 forming in the flask, and slowly, the distillate started to enter the condenser, and then the receiving flask. The process went on for about 2 hours, in which a sufficient amount of Br2 was collected.

The next step was to safely ampoule the bromine. The ampoule was created using a glass test tube, heated with a torch and carefully stretched to form a neck. The bromine was then added carefully using a glass funnel, and the test tube was fully sealed, forming an ampoule full of bromine.

The ampoule was then safely stored within protective packaging, within another glass container, and put inside a chemical storage refrigerator for long-term storage.
